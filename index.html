Chapter 1: Operations

The engine room of the business: Transforming inputs into outputs.

1. Role of Operations Management

The Strategic Role

Operations is about business goals. It's not just making things; it's about cost leadership and product differentiation.

  • Cost Leadership: Aiming to have the lowest costs in the industry (e.g., Aldi).
  • Differentiation: Distinguishing products (goods or services) in some way from competitors (e.g., Apple).

Interdependence

Operations does not work in a silo. It relies on:

  • Marketing: To determine what to produce and how much (based on research).
  • Finance: For budgets to buy machinery and pay staff.
  • HR: To hire and train the staff required for production.

2. Influences on Operations

External factors that force operations managers to adapt.

Globalisation

Access to global supply chains and markets. Reduces cost but increases logistical complexity.

Technology

CAM (Computer Aided Manufacturing) and robotics increase precision and speed but require high capital investment.

Quality Expectations

Customers have defined standards for durability and reliability that operations must meet.

Legal & Gov

WHS laws, anti-discrimination, and environmental regulations (e.g., Carbon tax debates).

3. Operations Processes

The "Input-Transformation-Output" model.

A. Inputs

Transformed Resources: Changed by the process (Materials, Information, Customers).

Transforming Resources: Do the changing (Human Resources, Facilities).

→

B. Transformation

The "doing" phase. Includes the 4 Vs (Volume, Variety, Variation in Demand, Visibility).

→

C. Outputs

The final good or service, plus customer service and warranties.

4. Operations Strategies

Performance Objectives

Use the acronym Q.S.D.F.C.C:

  • Quality: Is it good?
  • Speed: Is it fast?
  • Dependability: Is it on time?
  • Flexibility: Can we change it?
  • Customisation: Is it unique to the customer?
  • Cost: Is it cheap to make?
